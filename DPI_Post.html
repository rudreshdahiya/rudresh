DPI can’t hear you until it speaks your language

Published on July 12, 2025
Digital Public Infrastructure

The ocean we never learned to sail

On a muggy evening in Andhra Pradesh, Angamma, a 62-year-old farmer, holds up a smartphone and describes her chest pain to a Telugu-speaking chatbot. Within minutes, a cardiologist 600 km away reviews her vitals and schedules an urgent visit to the nearest district hospital. No hospital forms in English, no mistranslated symptoms, just care, delivered in the language she has spoken all her life.

Angamma’s story doesn’t fit the dominant narrative of India’s digital public infrastructure (DPI). Aadhaar gave her an ID, UPI let her children pay school fees in seconds, yet neither could listen to her. India’s fintech and e-governance stack skim only the surface of a vast linguistic ocean. As AI engineers working on India’s language stack like to joke, we’ve mapped barely 2% of India’s data seas; the rest lies in 122 major languages and 19,500 dialects. This cartographic gap is costing growth.

UPI was the last GDP accelerator. What’s next?

UPI’s flywheel, zero-cost rails, QR ubiquity, and government subsidies, added an estimated 0.03 percentage points to GDP for every 1% jump in transaction volume. But subsidies are thinning, MDR waivers are under review, and payment processors bled ₹2,500 cr last year.

The new accelerator isn’t another payment rail. It is comprehension, a Language DPI that lets every Indian transact, heal and borrow in their tongue, while AI agents do the heavy lifting in the background.

Two engines, one layer

Growth engine Today’s friction How Language DPI + GenAI removes it
Last-mile healthcare Tele-consults stall when patient, nurse and EMR “speak” diff­erent tongues. Raises misdiagnosis risk. Real-time speech-to-speech triage; auto-translated EMRs; dialect-aware symptom prompts.
MSME credit & insurance 40-50% of new-to-credit borrowers fail KYC or drop off digital forms written in English/Hindi. Vernacular onboarding, voice-driven GST & Aadhaar fetch, AI credit scores from cash-flow data.

Why existing DPI isn’t enough

  1. Account Aggregator pipes data but can’t interpret a Marathi GST narrative.
  2. ULI (Unified Lending Interface) pilots process APIs, not accents.
  3. CoWIN, ABDM store health data in English templates, losing clinical nuance when a Khasi patient describes breathlessness.

A Language DPI would sit beneath every other rail, a sovereign “context API” that standardises speech, text and intent across dialects, scripts and evolving slang. Think “Bhashini inside” every app, every form, every IVR.

How do we know its working?

Some metrics that can help trace the efficiency of Language DPI

Metric What It Measures How to Compute Feasibility Risks & Caveats
Language Mismatch Escalations How often patients request a switch in language during AI or telehealth interaction % of sessions where users switch language or abandon due to language mismatch (from app logs or IVR) Easy Might also reflect dialect or accent issues, not formal language mismatch alone
Prompt Repeat Rate Friction due to misunderstanding in AI voice interfaces (e.g. symptom checkers, IVR forms) Avg. no. of times a user has to repeat inputs per session (from speech recognition logs) Medium May be affected by background noise or device quality, not just language model
Form Completion Time (Delta) Whether vernacular interfaces reduce time taken to fill critical forms (KYC, insurance, onboarding) Avg. time taken to complete form in vernacular – Avg. time taken in English/Hindi Easy May vary with digital literacy and device type, not just language clarity
Repayment Reminder Acknowledgement Whether vernacular reminders improve engagement with financial notifications % of SMS/IVR reminders acknowledged or responded to within 24 hrs (segmented by language) Medium Requires basic engagement tracking setup; may be skewed by network/device issues

What’s in it for Private Sector?

TLDR: A competitive moat that only a few can see right now and a compliance head start that saves future cash out flows

I choose Banks and Hospitals because they have a unique value chains but yet there are some elements that match. A common thing is most customers don’t enjoy visiting banks or hospitals which is why you see these entities reaching out to you today though a digital mode in your mobile phones. Both entities require you to go through an extensive registration process, asking you for many proofs, that you don’t wish to repeat unless it’s urgent. Both use the once done registration process data to profile you and cater to you product/services that would be best suited for you. Ultimately, this product/service and consumer need fit decides how likely the customer is to continue the relationship with the entity in future.

A Language DPI is a way for these entities fund localization first lock in data moats: richer, labeled vernacular transcripts that recursively sharpen their models. Late movers face a cold-start spiral, no users, no dialect data, no accuracy. Regulators are quietly nudging this path. Draft Health-AI guidelines propose 98% accuracy for medical translations; RBI’s algorithmic credit norms will require model explainability in the borrower’s language. So more than anytime in past, compliance turns into competitive edge.

The flywheel

  1. Government seeds GPUs + open LLM weights.
  2. Start-ups build dialect adapters; Banks and Hospitals integrate and generate outcome data.
  3. Better data → better models → stricter regulators → higher switching costs.
  4. The result is a virtuous loop reminiscent of UPI’s early years, only this time, the product is context.

Will English lose out?

Unlikely. English’s gift is plasticity, Hinglish memes mutate hourly on Instagram. The Language DPI will embraces that reality, English becomes just another adapter, not the gatekeeper.

Why now?

If you think of it, it wasn’t UPI alone that could have made this impact we see today, it was actually the QR code scanner, simplifying the task of payment, and the Sound Box, removing the manual labour needed to add trust. Combined with the UPI infra, this trio made it all possible. The future follows the same first principles:removing friction with unified syntax-led communication. But the trust factor, now that’s where regulation needs to step up.

The place where banks meet hospitals most is insurance. And the last thing we’d want is differently priced premiums for the same context, due to hidden biases in risk scoring. Hence, regulation needs to be in the passenger seat with the innovation, not in the backseat. If done right, this can extend life, credit, and voice to the 1 billion Indians still stranded on the far side of the linguistic divide.

Angamma won’t read this story. But if the submarine we build reaches every dialectic trench, her grandchildren might write the next one, in a language we haven’t even trained on yet.
